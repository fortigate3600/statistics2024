Independance and Uncorrelation

Dependence and correlation are fundamental statistical concepts often used to describe the relationship between two variables, but they have substantial differences. Dependence indicates any kind of relationship between two variables: one variable depends on the other in a deterministic or probabilistic way. For example, there is dependence between height and weight, as a change in one often affects the other, although not always in a perfectly predictable manner. Correlation, on the other hand, is a specific measure of the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables; it takes values between -1 and +1, with -1 representing a perfect negative correlation, +1 representing a perfect positive correlation, and 0 indicating no linear correlation. However, it is important to note that correlation does not imply causality, and there can be dependence without linear correlation, as in cases of non-linear relationships. The most common measures for quantifying correlation include the Pearson coefficient, useful for linear relationships, and the Spearman coefficient, which is better suited for monotonic relationships that are not necessarily linear.
