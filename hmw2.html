Behavior of the Mean and Variance Over Time

The behavior of the mean and variance over time exhibits several interesting characteristics. In a series of repeated experiments or trials, the mean tends to stabilize around a constant value. This becomes particularly evident as the number of trials increases. As we increase the number of trials, the sample mean approaches the expected theoretical mean. On the other hand, the variance tends to decrease as the number of trials grows. Initially, when only a few trials are performed, the observations may vary significantly, leading to a higher variance. However, as more data is collected, the variance reduces, indicating that the observations are becoming increasingly concentrated around the mean.

In this context, it is important to note that, in independent trials, the mean remains stable while the variance tends to decrease. This implies that although the mean does not change, the uncertainty associated with the results decreases as the number of trials increases. As a result, the precision of the estimates improves with more data, which is a fundamental principle in statistics.

The reduction in variance over time is a key aspect of the law of large numbers. This law asserts that as the number of trials grows, the sample mean converges to the true population mean, and the variability around this mean decreases. Essentially, the more trials conducted, the more accurate and reliable the estimation of the population mean becomes.

Differences Between the Absolute Number of Successes and Relative Frequencies Distributions

The differences between the distribution of the absolute number of successes and that of relative frequencies are also significant. The distribution of the absolute number of successes represents the total number of successes obtained over a certain number of trials. Its variability depends on the total number of trials and the probability of success in each trial. For instance, consider an experiment with a success probability of 70%. The absolute number of successes can vary greatly from one experiment to another unless a large number of trials is conducted.

In contrast, relative frequencies represent the proportion of successes relative to the total number of trials, and they tend to stabilize more quickly compared to the distribution of the absolute number of successes. This occurs because, as mentioned earlier, the mean of the relative frequencies approaches the theoretical probability of success with an increasing number of trials.

A key aspect of this distinction is the asymptotic behavior: while the distribution of the absolute number of successes can exhibit considerable variability, the distribution of relative frequencies tends to converge toward the expected value, which is the probability of success. This phenomenon is described by the Central Limit Theorem, which states that the sum (or mean) of a large number of independent random variables tends to follow a normal distribution, regardless of the original distribution of the trials.

The Central Limit Theorem is a fundamental result in statistics, providing a foundation for making inferences about populations based on sample data. It explains why, with a sufficient number of trials, the distribution of relative frequencies will approach the true probability of success, making statistical predictions more reliable as the sample size increases.
