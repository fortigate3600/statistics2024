General Concept of Sampling Mean and Variance and Main Features of Their Distributions

The sampling mean and variance are fundamental concepts in statistics that provide insight into the characteristics of a dataset drawn from a population. The sampling mean is the average of the observed values in a sample, serving as an unbiased estimator of the population mean under random sampling. The sampling variance quantifies the spread of the data in the sample, representing the variability of observations around the sampling mean. Importantly, when samples are independently and identically distributed, the sampling distribution of the mean approximates a normal distribution as sample size increases, as described by the Central Limit Theorem. This property underpins many statistical methods and ensures reliable inference, with smaller variance for larger samples, highlighting the trade-off between precision and sample size.

General Idea of Lebesgue–Stieltjes Integration and Applications to Probability and Measure Theory

The Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral generalizes the Riemann integral, extending its applicability to more complex functions and measures. It is defined with respect to a non-decreasing function, allowing integration in terms of arbitrary measures rather than just length or volume. In probability theory, the Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral is used to define expectations of random variables concerning cumulative distribution functions, accommodating both discrete and continuous distributions. In measure theory, it serves as a cornerstone for unifying different notions of integration, enabling the treatment of singular and mixed measures. This versatility makes it a powerful tool for rigorous mathematical analysis across disciplines such as stochastic processes, functional analysis, and financial mathematics.
