The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is a cornerstone of mathematical analysis, establishing a profound connection between the concepts of differentiation and integration. It provides the theoretical foundation for integral calculus and plays a critical role in a wide variety of mathematical and applied fields. The theorem consists of two key parts. The first part asserts that the definite integral of a continuous function 𝑓(𝑥) over a closed interval [𝑎, 𝑏] can be computed using an antiderivative 𝐹(𝑥) of the function 𝑓(𝑥). Specifically, it states that: ∫ₐᵇ 𝑓(𝑥) dx = 𝐹(𝑏) − 𝐹(𝑎). This means that the integral of 𝑓(𝑥) can be evaluated by finding the difference between the values of its antiderivative 𝐹(𝑥) at the boundaries of the interval. The second part of the theorem establishes that if 𝐹(𝑥) is defined as the integral of 𝑓(𝑥) from a fixed point 𝑎, then the derivative of 𝐹(𝑥) with respect to 𝑥 is equal to the function 𝑓(𝑥), i.e., 𝐹′(𝑥) = 𝑓(𝑥). This shows the reverse relationship between differentiation and integration, providing a solid link between the two concepts.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is not only foundational to pure mathematics but also has significant applications in various scientific disciplines, including probability theory. In the context of probability, it helps define the relationship between the probability density function (PDF) and the cumulative distribution function (CDF). Specifically, if we have a continuous random variable 𝑋 with a probability density function 𝑓(𝑥), the cumulative distribution function 𝐹(𝑥) gives the probability that the random variable 𝑋 takes a value less than or equal to a specific value 𝑥. This is expressed mathematically as: 𝐹(𝑥) = 𝑃(𝑋 ≤ 𝑥) = ∫₋∞ˣ 𝑓(𝑡) dt. In this framework, the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus implies that the derivative of the CDF 𝐹(𝑥) with respect to 𝑥 is equal to the PDF 𝑓(𝑥). That is, 𝐹′(𝑥) = 𝑓(𝑥). This relationship shows how the PDF represents the local "density" of probability at each point, while the CDF accumulates this density along the entire range of values that 𝑋 can take. Essentially, the CDF gives the cumulative probability up to a certain point, while the PDF describes how this probability is distributed across different values of 𝑋. The Fundamental Theorem, in this case, helps bridge the connection between the two functions and provides a deeper understanding of the underlying distribution of the random variable.
