The Median as the Optimal Location Statistic: Exploring Centrality Measures and Their Generalizations

Median as the Minimum of the Sum of Absolute Deviations

To illustrate why the median minimizes the sum of absolute deviations, let’s consider a set of data points x1, x2, ..., xn where x1 ≤ x2 ≤ ... ≤ xn. The goal is to find a value c that minimizes the sum of absolute deviations: 

                      S(c) = ∑i=1n |xi - c|

The sum of absolute deviations measures how far each data point xi is from a central value c. To find the value of c that minimizes this sum, we need to consider how S(c) behaves as c changes.

  • When c is less than x1, all terms in the sum |xi - c| are decreasing as c increases, but the decrease in the sum slows down as we approach the median.
  • When c is greater than xn, all terms in the sum |xi - c| are increasing as c increases, but the increase in the sum slows down as we move further from the median.
  • At the value of c that is the median, the sum of deviations is minimized because this point balances the number of terms on either side, leading to the smallest possible sum of absolute distances.

Mathematically, the median c is the value that satisfies the condition where the number of points less than or equal to c is at least as large as the number of points greater than or equal to c. This minimizes the total distance, hence making the median the optimal location statistic in terms of minimizing absolute deviations.

Different Conceptual Definitions of a "Location" Statistic

A "location" statistic (also called a "center" or "central tendency") is a measure that characterizes the center of a distribution. There are several ways to define such a statistic, each capturing different aspects of the data's central position. The generalization of these ideas can lead to a vast number of different definitions. Here are a few conceptual definitions:

  • Mean (Arithmetic Average): The arithmetic mean is the sum of all values divided by the number of values. It represents the "balance point" of the distribution, where the sum of the deviations from the mean is zero. The mean is sensitive to extreme values (outliers) and works well for symmetric, normally distributed data.
  • Median (Middle Value): The median is the middle value when the data is ordered. It represents the point that divides the data into two equal parts. The median is robust to outliers because it is only dependent on the order of the data, not the values themselves.
  • Mode (Most Frequent Value): The mode is the value that occurs most frequently in the dataset. It is useful in describing distributions with distinct peaks or multiple clusters. For multimodal distributions, there can be multiple modes.
  • Trimean: The trimean is a weighted average of the median and the midrange (the average of the minimum and maximum values). It combines aspects of both the center and the spread of the data, providing a more robust location measure.
  • Geometric Mean: The geometric mean is the nth root of the product of all values. It is often used for data with exponential growth or when values are multiplied together (e.g., in financial or population growth data). It emphasizes the central tendency in multiplicative processes.
  • Harmonic Mean: The harmonic mean is the reciprocal of the arithmetic mean of the reciprocals of the data points. It is useful when the data represents rates or ratios and can give a better measure of centrality in certain situations (e.g., average speed).
  • Weighted Mean: The weighted mean is a mean where each data point is given a weight, indicating its importance. It generalizes the arithmetic mean by allowing different data points to contribute differently to the central value.
  • Quantiles and Percentiles: Quantiles divide the data into equal parts, and percentiles specify the value below which a given percentage of observations fall. These statistics generalize the concept of the median, providing more nuanced measures of centrality and spread.
  • M-estimators: These are a general class of location estimators that minimize a function of the deviations of the data points. The choice of the function (e.g., absolute deviations, squared deviations, or others) leads to different types of location statistics, such as the median (absolute deviation) or the mean (squared deviation).
  • Robust Location Measures: These are defined to minimize the effect of outliers. Examples include the median, the trimmed mean (where a certain percentage of extreme values are removed), and the Hampel identifier. These are often used when the data contains outliers that could distort other location statistics.
